Food complaint


Use this complaint type to report:

  • A food business that is not licensed
  • A food business that is not displaying a current licence

  Before you continue

Not all food businesses in Queensland need a licence. A food business does not need a licence for the sale of:

  • packaged food
  • whole fruit and vegetables
  • drinks (except fruit and vegetable juice processed on site)
  • ice, including flavoured ice
  • seeds, spices, dried herbs, tea leaves, coffee or ground coffee
  • unpackaged snack food (for example, confectionary, potato chips, corn chips or nuts)
  • food sold by non-profit organisations for fundraising (for example, sausage sizzle or cake stall)
  • food provided at state government run facilities (for example, a public hospital or state school)

Licensed food businesses are required by law to display their licence or a copy of their licence so that customers can easily see it. The expiry date is shown on the licence so you can see if the licence is current.


The common symptoms of food poisoning include fever, headache, nausea, vomiting, abdominal cramps or diarrhoea. These may start within hours of eating but can sometimes take days or weeks before you being to feel sick.

Foreign matter is defined as anything introduced to a food product at any point in its production or distribution that you believe doesn’t belong there. Foreign matter is usually as a result of a breakdown in the processing or handling of the food and is not intentional. Investigation is usually required to work out how the object got into the food.


  Intentional contamination

If you suspect a food product has been intentionally contaminated call 13 HEALTH (13 43 25 84).

Intentional contamination of food means contamination by a person who intends to contaminate the food, including by adding, mixing or putting a harmful or poisonous substance or thing into the food.

  Tongs, gloves and hairnets

Food handlers do not have to use tongs or wear gloves or hairnets when handling food.

All food handlers must prevent the contamination of food, but how they do this is up to them. For example, if they wash their hands regularly, in between tasks and each time they handle food, they don’t need to wear gloves.

For further information read about the Responsibilities of food handlers.


  Footwear

Workplace Health and Safety Queensland are responsible for the regulation of hazards in a workplace and how to prevent workers being injured through suitable controls, for example non-slip footwear.

Complaints relating to the footwear of a food handler cannot be investigated by Queensland Health and should be reported directly to the OLGR through their website.

Food labels must show a food name. For example, strawberry yoghurt must contain strawberries. If the yoghurt contained strawberry flavouring rather than real fruit, then the name should be called strawberry-flavoured yoghurt.

Food labels may also have a description to describe the food. If the name of the food does not reflect its true nature, then a description should be included on the label. For example, the food name Luke’s Hot Sauce not does reflect the true nature of the food. A description should be added to the label to say that it is a Red hot chilli pepper sauce.

A health star rating is a rating system that assess the nutritional profile of packaged food and gives the food a rating from ½ star to 5 stars.

The Australian Department of Health is responsible for handling any enquiries or complaints about the health star rating system. If your complaint is about the health star rating on a label, contact the Australian Department of Health using the contact details from their website.

A food label must include a statement of ingredients, including any additives or alternative ingredients.

The ingredients must be listed in descending order of ingoing weight. This means, if an ingredient is listed as the first ingredient in the list, then the food contains more of this ingredient than any other ingredient.

Some food must include warning or advisory statements on the label.

For example, food containing royal jelly must be labelled with the statement:

‘This product contains royal jelly which has been reported to cause severe allergic reactions and in rare cases, fatalities, especially in asthma and allergy sufferers’.

Food retailers must not alter, remove, erase, destroy or obscure the label on the package unless permission has been given by Queensland Health.

Packaged food can be re-labelled if the label containedhas incorrect information. The new label must be attached securely and completely cover the incorrect information.


  Some foods do not need a label

Some food in Queensland may not need a label. For example, when the food is:

  • not in a package
  • sold at a fundraising event
  • packaged in the presence of the purchaser
  • made and packaged on the premises from where it is sold
  • delivered packaged and ready for consumption at the express order of the purchaser (for example, take away pizza), except when the food is sold from a vending machine
  • displayed in an assisted service display cabinet.
  • whole or cut fresh fruit and vegetables (other than seed sprouts or similar products) in a package that does not obscure their nature or quality

A food label must include a nutrition information panel in a standard format and include details like the serving size, energy, protein, fat (total and saturated), carbohydrates (and sugars) and sodium.

Use By dates and Best Before dates are compulsory for all packaged food with a shelf life of 2 years or less.

For bread with a shelf life of less than 7 days, the best before date may be replaced by a Baked For date or Baked On date.

It is not safe to eat food after the Use By date. Food must not be sold after its Use By date. Foods with a Baked For date can be sold and eaten past the Baked For date .

The National Measurement Institute (NMI) is responsible for making sure food is measured and weighed correctly, and that the food is labelled with the correct weight and unit. For example, food labelled as being 500g should weigh 500g.

Complaints relating to the weights and other measures on foods cannot be investigated by Queensland Health and should be reported directly to the NMI through their website.
